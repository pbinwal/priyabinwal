<span class="thought-title">Thought:</span> Do you know what's common between a 19-year-old alpha-bro posting a story about their post-workout meal on instagram and a 49-year-old Indian uncle madly blaring horns on the streets of New Delhi? 1. Both involve two people pushing a button to desperately tell a world that doesn't care— "I exist". 2. Both occur more frequently than one would like to think.<br><br>


Dear gentle reader,<br><br>


Know this: if you can hold a plank for a duration that is longer than your attention span, there is indeed something <em>special</em> about you. Either you have a <em>very</em> strong core, and if that's the case then I say good for you because that pilates class finally seems to be working (or is it the protein shakes?), OR you've messed up with your brain in ways that even science is just beginning to comprehend. If you happen to be alive at the time of writing, the odds weigh overwhelmingly in favour of the second possibility. Don't believe me? Plank. Now.<br><br>


Now maybe you say– well, Priya, we've heard all of this before, but it's not easy for Belle to leave the castle once she has fallen in love with the beast, and maybe the beast isn't even a <em>real</em> beast but just royalty trapped in a fortress of their own, and to that I say buddy <em>you do you</em> even if that means being <em>delulu</em>. But if you, like me, are seeking some sanity in a world full of delusions and distractions, here are my two cents for you. Here’s my experience of downloading the instagram app and deleting it within 48 hours because I couldn't, <em>wouldn't</em>, stand it.<br><br>


Last weekend, I created an instagram account for the first time in my life (a little late to the party as a person who is technically Gen Z and in their twenties at the time of writing, eh?). The move wasn't spontaneous but well deliberated. For an <s>artist</s> novice who is just beginning to find her voice and figuring out how to be heard amid all the cacophony, social media (or instagram in particular) is the perfect siren. It beckons to you with all its all-to-perfect promises of finding a platform, a community, or at the very least, not feeling left out (FOMO, YOLO, what are they calling it these days?) and like every foolish fisherman, you follow. So the siren called and I answered by creating an insta account through my web browser and making a couple posts about loosely written <em>shayari</em> (couplets) that I had composed sometime over the past one year. Mind you, we haven’t gotten to the app yet. Just my humble browser so far.<br><br>


The day after I created an account on instagram, two friends who have been part of my close circle of comrades for as long as I can remember texted me saying how surprised they were that I had finally <em>done it</em>. I have to say, I was both a little amused and mildly offended that it got the reaction it did, which, in my opinion, should have been warranted only if I had voluntarily decided to  get high (it has never happened). Or perhaps those in the trenches of social-engagement <em>do</em> truly regard those who choose to forego it as utter social failures. (That is a badge I would happily don– to date some of my favourite words from any human to exist remain— <em>it is no measure of health to be well-adjusted to a profoundly sick society</em>, by Jiddu Krishnamurty. But we digress.) In any case, I conveyed my amusement to my friends, told them how googling <em>‘how to make a post on instagram’</em> in 2026 made me feel older, chuckled to myself, and soldiered on.<br><br>


As someone who got onto this beloved app of billions so late, the multitude of customisable settings seemed confusing. The only thing clear to me after two days of experimentation was this— their website <em>sucks</em>. I am not going into the details of what errors and inconveniences I encountered as I forged my way through, but you are welcome to be my guest and try. And this is not coming from a technologically incompetent boomer complaining about technology 101s, but from someone who codes for a living as part of her job (with a lot of help from AI of course ;). Long story short, I can figure out websites if they are well made, and the one I found myself on certainly wasn't.<br><br>


So on a Monday evening, as I geared myself to post yet another <em>shayari</em>, I had two options. Grapple with the website as I had done for two days or download the instagram app (does Gen Alpha know that app is short for application?). Well, the app in question has been around for most of my adolescent and young-adult years, and like all things that most humans are hyped-up about, I have always been a little suspicious of it. What can I say, that's how the cynical mind works. You see the herd joyously heading towards the grass and you're convinced there's a wolf lurking behind it. I've always believed that while it's possible the makers of social media may have had as noble intentions as it is possible for a bunch of college-dropouts to have, all of those intentions have been forgotten along the way to the effect that what remains now is only a cadaver of the original idea taxidermied with capitalism. A lot of CAPITALISM (see what I did there?).<br><br>


But no matter how suspicious a cynic, she must sometimes succumb to the general incompetence of web developers. (Or maybe it's not their incompetence after all but capitalism at play again, because they really, <em>desperately</em> want you to have the app that helps them make billions. See? I am a cynic). And so one fine Monday evening in April 2026, I found myself downloading the app with a pout on my face. And then? A whole new world. No, that sounds too positive. A whole new rabbit's hole. That's more like it.<br><br>


Social media engineers and designers— I cannot overstate what a fantastic job you've done in turning all of us into lab rats. What can I say? That the design, the colours, the way the whole interface orgasms upon the gentle touch of your fingers is so well thought of? That you, some of the smartest people in the world, have managed to create something that is making the rest of humanity, which by the way includes you, all the dumber? I could rave on about this, but chances are, that you know all this already and there's nothing new for you there. What I <em>can</em> tell from my experience with the Pandora's box is that even though I didn't scroll a single reel and mostly minded my business by posting my <em>shayari</em> and taking a quick look at some friends’ stories, I could <em>feel</em> my brain chemistry changing. Okay that very clearly is an euphemism, but you know what I mean. I could <em>feel</em> the urge to go back to the app and… and do what? Honestly I don't know. It wasn't a need to do anything in particular but more of a need to do <em>something</em>. As I said, I didn't scroll a single reel so it's not like 48 hours (more like two evenings) somehow turned me into an addict. But I could feel that if I stayed on this path, the only destination was turning into one <em>bit by bit</em>. And the odds were that if I wasn't observant enough of my behavioural patterns as I generally am, I wouldn't even know that it had happened.<br><br>


And so after 48 hours of experimenting with the siren and almost jumping off the ship of sanity, I made a Ulysses pact. To the unaware, Ulysses was a Greek hero who also once found himself aboard a ship that encountered a siren as it sailed. He knew that men often gave in to the siren’s beckoning when they found themselves in such a predicament and to prevent that fate from befalling him, he asked his companions to tie him to the mast so that he could still listen to the siren's song without risking his life in a moment of bad judgement. (That had better have been a <em>really</em> good song). Inspired by this story, a Ulysses pact refers to a self-binding pact made by one's free will, in acknowledgement of the fact that adverse or enticing circumstances often rob one of one's rationality. Or as the hindi poet Dinkar so wisely said in <em>Rashmirathi</em>— जब नाश मनुज पर छाता है, पहले विवेक मर जाता है।<br><br>


And so, acknowledging that even the most rational and strong-willed people can be swayed when it comes to evading the callous enticements that the capitalistic machinery sends our way— my Ulysses pact to me is to delete the app, but keep the account. This means that every time I have to post my <em>shayari</em>, I have to go use my laptop (the pact includes no phone access). Remember how they say social media exploits some of the most crude vulnerabilities of human beings such as the need for social approval? In answer to that I have placed my bets on another fact about humans that I <em>know</em> to be true— <span style="color: #2d7a70; font-weight: bold; text-transform: uppercase;">HUMAN BEINGS ARE ONE LAZY SPECIES</span>. And as a proud member of the species <em>sapiens</em>— so am I. Which means going through the extra effort of leaving the warm cocoon of my blanket, finding my laptop, finding the charger, establishing a working connection between the charger and the laptop and the charger and the socket, waiting for Windows to boot, waiting for the terrible wifi to be available, then giving up and reaching out for the LAN cable, opening the browser and waiting for it to load, logging-in on insta, and <em>then</em> making a post leaves little room for addiction. The hope is that, much like Ulysses, I would still be able to listen to the song of the siren <em>at my will</em> without falling prey to it in moments without clarity.<br><br>


So yeah, my plan is to combat years of research on reward circuits and app optimization with human laziness. Genius, isn't it? XD<br><br>
